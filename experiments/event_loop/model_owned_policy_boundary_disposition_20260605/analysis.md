## Interpretation

This connects the live policy-selection result to the event-loop substrate.
Before this panel, a model could choose `stop_complete` or
`ask_external_evidence`, but the scheduler mostly represented that as ordinary
idle completion. Now those choices become explicit append-only sidecar data.

The design distinction held:

- lifecycle status answers "what happened to the event execution?";
- policy disposition answers "what control-loop outcome did this wake choose?"

That separation matters. A completed wake can mean "continued", "finished", or
"blocked on missing evidence"; those are materially different scheduler
outcomes even though the event lifecycle status is `completed` in all three
cases.

## Notable Observation

The continue branch now emits two policy dispositions per full chain:

1. first wake: `continue_after`, classified `continued`, linked to the pending
   continuation event;
2. follow-up wake: `stop_complete`, classified `complete`.

That is exactly what we want for a chain-level audit trail. The sidecar shows
the transition from "continue this work" to "this work is now complete" without
overloading event lifecycle state.

## Caveats

This remains a small single-model panel. It shows the new disposition substrate
works on live Deepseek output for this narrow policy-boundary task. It does not
establish broad provider or prompt robustness.

The policy action vocabulary is still fixed to three actions:
`continue_after`, `stop_complete`, and `ask_external_evidence`. Expanding the
control-loop vocabulary should be preregistered and added deliberately to the
event-store validation.

## Design Implication

The event-loop scheduler now has three layers of observable state:

1. lifecycle events: pending, running, completed, failed, expired, suppressed;
2. continuation events: new scheduled work generated by a completed wake;
3. policy dispositions: the model-owned control decision made by a wake.

This gives us enough structure to ask a more interesting scheduler question:
what should the loop do with evidence-blocked work? It can now be detected
directly rather than inferred.

## Next Research Move

The next useful falsification target is evidence-request handling:

- when a wake emits `ask_external_evidence`, can the scheduler turn the
  disposition into a first-class external-evidence request or blocked-work
  record?
- can that record be resumed later when evidence arrives?
- can the resumed wake distinguish newly supplied evidence from the prior
  missing-evidence state?

This would move the loop from "observe blocked work" to "manage blocked work".
